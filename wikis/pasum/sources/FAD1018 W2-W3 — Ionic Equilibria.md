## 1. Introduction to Ionic Equilibria

**Ionic equilibria** refer to equilibrium reactions in aqueous solution or molten state that show the presence of ions.

- Reaction contains positively charged ions (**cations**) and negatively charged ions (**anions**).
- **Strong acids/bases:** fully ionised / dissociate
- **Weak acids/bases:** partially dissociate

### Electrolytes

Ionic equilibria are fundamental to electrolysis and electrochemistry:

- NaCl(s) ‚ÄĒ(őĒ)‚Üí Na‚Āļ(‚Ąď) + Cl‚ĀĽ(‚Ąď) (molten)

Applications include electrolytic cells and Daniell cells (galvanic cells).

## 4. Theories of Acid and Base

There are three main theories relating to acids and bases:

1. **Arrhenius Theory (A)**
2. **Br√łnsted-Lowry Theory (B-L)**
3. **Lewis Theory (L)**

### 4.1 Arrhenius Theory (1884)

Introduced by **Svante Arrhenius** (1884).

**Acid:** A substance that dissociates in water to produce an excess of hydrogen ions, H‚Āļ(aq) [or hydronium H‚āÉO‚Āļ] in aqueous solution.

Compounds (acids) dissolve in water in two ways:

1. **Dissociates without reacting with water molecules:**
   $$\text{HCl(aq)} \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \text{H}^+\text{(aq)} + \text{Cl}^-\text{(aq)}$$

2. **Dissociates when reacted with water molecules:**
   $$\text{HCl(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}(\ell) \rightarrow \text{H}_3\text{O}^+\text{(aq)} + \text{Cl}^-\text{(aq)}$$

**Base:** A substance that dissociates in water to produce an excess of hydroxide ions, OH‚ĀĽ(aq) in aqueous solution.

$$\text{NaOH(aq)} \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}} \text{Na}^+\text{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-\text{(aq)}$$

> **Limitation:** Applicable only to aqueous solution for compounds containing H‚Āļ and OH‚ĀĽ.

### 4.2 Br√łnsted-Lowry Theory (1923)

Proposed by **Johannes Br√łnsted** and **Thomas M. Lowry** (1923).

**Acid:**
- Are **proton (H‚Āļ) donor**
- Becomes its **conjugate base** when it donates a proton

**Base:**
- Are **proton (H‚Āļ) acceptor**
- Becomes its **conjugate acid** when it accepts a proton

**Example 1:**
$$\text{HCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{Cl}^- + \text{H}_3\text{O}^+$$
- HCl / Cl‚ĀĽ is called a **conjugate pair**
- H‚āāO / H‚āÉO‚Āļ is also called a **conjugate pair**

**Example 2:**
$$\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{NH}_3 \rightleftharpoons \text{OH}^- + \text{NH}_4^+$$
- H‚āāO acts as Br√łnsted-Lowry Acid (donates proton)
- NH‚āÉ acts as Br√łnsted-Lowry Base (accepts proton)

**Example 3:**
$$\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow \text{HSO}_4^- + \text{H}_3\text{O}^+$$
- H‚āāSO‚āĄ is a strong acid, diprotic acid (dissociates 2 H‚Āļ)

#### Water as Amphoteric Solvent

- H‚āāO is an **amphoteric solvent**
- Acting both as a base and an acid
- It undergoes **auto-ionisation** (self-ionisation):

$$\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_3\text{O}^+ + \text{OH}^-$$

### 4.3 Lewis Theory (1938)

Proposed by **G. N. Lewis** (1938).

**Acid (Lewis acid):**
- Is an atom, ion or molecule that **accepts a pair of electrons** to form a coordinate covalent bond
- **Electrophile** (usually +ve charge)
- Examples: BF‚āÉ, AlCl‚āÉ (incomplete octet)

```smiles
Cl[Al](Cl)Cl
```
**Base (Lewis base):**
- Is an atom, ion or molecule that **donates a pair of electrons** to form a coordinate covalent bond
- **Nucleophile** (usually -ve charge)

**Examples:**

$$\text{NH}_3 + \text{BF}_3 \rightarrow \text{adduct}$$

```smiles
FB(F)F
```
```smiles
N
```
$$\text{H}_3\text{C-NH}_2 + \text{BH}_3 \rightarrow \text{H}_3\text{C-NH}_2-BH}_3$$

$$\text{H}_3\text{C-NH}_2 + \text{HCl} \rightarrow \text{H}_3\text{C-NH}_3\text{Cl}$$

$$\text{F}^- + \text{BF}_3 \rightarrow [\text{F-BF}_3]^-$$

$$2\text{NH}_3 + \text{Ag}^+ \rightarrow [\text{H}_3\text{N-Ag-NH}_3]^+$$

The products of the reactions are called **adducts**.

#### Lewis Base vs Lewis Acid Comparison

| Category | Lewis Base (e‚ĀĽ donor) | Lewis Acid (e‚ĀĽ acceptor) |
|----------|----------------------|-------------------------|
| General | Something that complexes with a Lewis Acid | Something that complexes with a Lewis Base |
| Lone-Pair | Lone-Pair donors: :NH‚āÉ, H‚āāO: | Lone-Pair acceptors: BF‚āÉ, AlCl‚āÉ |
| Br√łnsted relation | Br√łnsted bases: ‚ĀĽOH, ‚ĀĽCH‚āÉ | Metal cations: Al¬≥‚Āļ, Fe¬≥‚Āļ |
| Nucleophile/Electrophile | Nucleophiles: CH‚āÉ-S‚ĀĽ | Electrophiles: CH‚āÉCO‚Āļ |
| Ligands | [Fe(H‚āāO)‚āÜ]¬≥‚Āļ | The proton: H‚Āļ |
| Counter ions | Anionic counter ions: SO‚āĄ¬≤‚ĀĽ, NO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ | Cationic spectator ions: K‚Āļ |
| ŌÄ systems | Electron-rich ŌÄ systems (benzene) | Electron-poor ŌÄ systems: [CH‚āā-CH=CH‚āā]‚Āļ |

> **Important:** Not all Lewis Bases are Br√łnsted-Lowry Bases because the definition of a Lewis Base is broader than that of a Br√łnsted-Lowry Base.

## 5. Amphoteric Species

An **amphoteric species** is a substance that has the ability to act either as an acid or a base depending on what other substances it is reacting with.

- Acids donate protons (or accept electron pairs) when reacting with basic substances.
- Bases accept protons when reacting with acidic substances.

**Examples of amphoteric species:** water, aluminium hydroxide, bicarbonate ion.

Other amphoteric substances can be formed from the oxides and hydroxides of metals like beryllium, zinc, and lead.

**Amino acids**, which are the building blocks of proteins, are also amphoteric.

### 5.1 Water as Amphoteric Species

- Normally considered a neutral substance.
- If water reacts with a base like ammonia, it acts as an **acid** by donating a proton:

$$\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) + \text{NH}_3\text{(aq)} \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+\text{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-\text{(aq)}$$

- If water comes in contact with an acid like HCl, it acts as a **base** by receiving a proton:

$$\text{HCl(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow \text{Cl}^-\text{(aq)} + \text{H}_3\text{O}^+\text{(aq)}$$

**H‚āāO is an amphoteric species.**

### 5.2 Bicarbonate Ion (HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ) as Amphoteric Species

- When reacting with the acid hydronium ion, HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ acts as a **base** by accepting a proton:

$$\text{H}_3\text{O}^+\text{(aq)} + \text{HCO}_3^-\text{(aq)} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_2\text{CO}_3\text{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l)$$

- When reacting with the base hydroxide ion, HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ acts as an **acid** by donating a proton:

$$\text{HCO}_3^-\text{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-\text{(aq)} \rightleftharpoons \text{CO}_3^{2-}\text{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l)$$

**HCO‚āÉ‚ĀĽ is an amphoteric species.**

### 5.3 Aluminum Hydroxide (Al(OH)‚āÉ) as Amphoteric Species

- If it reacts with a base like NaOH, it acts as an **acid**:

$$\text{Al(OH)}_3\text{(aq)} + \text{NaOH(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na[Al(OH)}_4]\text{(aq)}$$

- With HCl, it acts as a **base**:

$$\text{Al(OH)}_3\text{(aq)} + 3\text{HCl(aq)} \rightleftharpoons \text{AlCl}_3\text{(aq)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{O}(l)$$

**Al(OH)‚āÉ is an amphoteric species.**

## 6. Strength of Acid & Base

### 6.1 Strong Acid

- Dissociate completely (100% ionized) in aqueous solution to form H‚Āļ or H‚āÉO‚Āļ ions.
- Examples: HCl(aq), H‚āāSO‚āĄ(aq), HNO‚āÉ(aq), HBr(aq), HClO‚āĄ(aq), HI(aq)

- The molarities of H‚Āļ or H‚āÉO‚Āļ ions are high for strong acids.
- pH 1 ‚Äď very acidic
- Low pH means high molarities of H‚Āļ or H‚āÉO‚Āļ ions
- The degree of dissociation ($\alpha$) for strong acid is 1 or 100%

$$\text{HCl(aq)} \rightarrow \text{H}^+\text{(aq)} + \text{Cl}^-\text{(aq)}$$

### 6.2 Strong Base

- Dissociate completely (100% ionized) in aqueous solution to form OH‚ĀĽ ion.
- Examples: NaOH(aq), Mg(OH)‚āā(aq), Ca(OH)‚āā(aq)



- The molarities of OH‚ĀĽ are high for strong bases.
- pH 14 ‚Äď very basic
- High pH means high molarities of OH‚ĀĽ ion
- The degree of dissociation ($\alpha$) for strong base is 1 or 100%

$$\text{NaOH(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+\text{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-\text{(aq)}$$

### 6.3 Weak Acid

- Not dissociate completely (partially ionized) in aqueous solution to form H‚Āļ or H‚āÉO‚Āļ ions.
- Therefore the concentrations of H‚Āļ or H‚āÉO‚Āļ ions in the solutions are always less than the concentrations of the original acids.
- Examples: CH‚āÉCOOH(aq), HCN(aq), HNO‚āā(aq), HF(aq)

- The system exists as an equilibrium between the un-dissociated acid and the dissociated ions.

$$\text{HA}_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(\ell)} \rightleftharpoons \text{A}^-_{\text{(aq)}} + \text{H}_3\text{O}^+_{\text{(aq)}}$$

At equilibrium: (1-x) M HA, x M A‚ĀĽ, x M H‚āÉO‚Āļ

$$K_a = \frac{[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}$$

- $K_a$ is a dissociation constant for acid, measures the acid strength.
- Values of $K_a$ are unaffected by the concentration of acids (only temperature ‚Äď Le Chatelier's principle).
- **Larger $K_a$ means stronger acid.**
- **Higher $K_a$ = lower $pK_a$**
- $pK_a = -\log K_a$

- Not dissociate completely (partially ionized) in aqueous solution to form OH‚ĀĽ ion.
- Therefore the concentrations of OH‚ĀĽ ions in the solutions are always less than the concentrations of the original bases.
- Examples: NH‚āĄOH(aq), CH‚āÉNH‚āā(aq), C‚āÖH‚āÖN(aq)

- The system exists as an equilibrium between the un-dissociated base and the dissociated ions.

$$\text{NH}_3\text{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}(\ell) \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+\text{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-\text{(aq)}$$

At equilibrium: (1-x) M NH‚āÉ, x M NH‚āĄ‚Āļ, x M OH‚ĀĽ

$$K_b = \frac{[\text{NH}_4^+][\text{OH}^-]}{[\text{NH}_3]}$$

- $K_b$ is a dissociation constant for base, measures the base strength.
- **Larger $K_b$ means stronger base.**
- **Higher $K_b$ = lower $pK_b$**
- $pK_b = -\log K_b$
- pH between 8 to 10

### Hydrolysis
Hydrolysis is the reaction of a cation or an anion (or both) with water. The reaction is reversible.

- Cation hydrolysis (produces H‚āÉO‚Āļ):
  $$\text{X}^+_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons \text{XOH} + \text{H}_3\text{O}^+_{(aq)}$$
- Anion hydrolysis (produces OH‚ĀĽ):
  $$\text{Y}^-_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons \text{HY} + \text{OH}^-_{(aq)}$$

### Relationship between Ka and Kb
For a conjugate acid-base pair:

$$K_a \times K_b = K_w = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \text{ (at 25¬įC)}$$

$$pK_a + pK_b = pK_w = 14$$

**Derivation:**
- Acid dissociation: $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_3\text{O}^+ + \text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-$
  $$K_a = \frac{[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-]}{[\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}]}$$
- Conjugate base hydrolysis: $\text{CH}_3\text{COO}^- + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{OH}^- + \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$
  $$K_b = \frac{[\text{OH}^-][\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}]}{[\text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-]}$$

Multiplying the two expressions: $K_a K_b = [\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{OH}^-] = K_w$.

### Neutral Salts (SA-SB)
Formed from strong acid + strong base. pH = 7.

- Neither cation nor anion reacts with water ‚Üí **no hydrolysis**
- Both ions are spectator ions (low charge density / low proton affinity)

$$\text{HCl}_{(aq)} + \text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{NaCl}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

$$\text{NaCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+_{(aq)} + \text{Cl}^-_{(aq)}$$

```smiles
[Na+].[Cl-]
```
```smiles
[K+].[Br-]
```
```smiles
[K+].[I-]
```
```smiles
[Rb+].[Br-]
```
```smiles
[Ba+2].[Cl-].[Cl-]
```
From ionic theory, cancelling common ions leaves $\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}^+ + \text{OH}^-$, so $[\text{H}^+] = [\text{OH}^-]$.

### Acidic Salts (SA-WB)
Formed from strong acid + weak base. pH < 7.

- **Cation undergoes hydrolysis** (donates proton to water)
- Examples: NH‚āĄCl, NH‚āĄNO‚āÉ

$$\text{HCl}_{(aq)} + \text{NH}_{3(aq)} \rightarrow \text{NH}_4\text{Cl}_{(aq)}$$

Dissociation:
$$\text{NH}_4\text{Cl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{NH}_4^+_{(aq)} + \text{Cl}^-_{(aq)}$$

Hydrolysis:
$$\text{NH}_4^+_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_{3(aq)} + \text{H}_3\text{O}^+_{(aq)}$$

```smiles
[NH4+].[Cl-]
```
```smiles
[NH4+].[O-][N+](=O)[O-]
```
#### Worked Example: pH of 0.20 M NH‚āĄCl
Given $K_b$ for NH‚āÉ = $1.8 \times 10^{-5}$:

1. $K_a = \frac{K_w}{K_b} = \frac{1.0 \times 10^{-14}}{1.8 \times 10^{-5}} = 5.6 \times 10^{-10}$
2. ICE table for NH‚āĄ‚Āļ hydrolysis:
   - Initial: [NH‚āĄ‚Āļ] = 0.20 M, [NH‚āÉ] = 0, [H‚āÉO‚Āļ] = 0
   - Change: ‚Äďx, +x, +x
   - Equilibrium: (0.20 ‚Äď x), x, x
3. $K_a = \frac{x^2}{0.20-x} \approx \frac{x^2}{0.20}$ (assuming x is small)
4. $x = [\text{H}_3\text{O}^+] = 1.05 \times 10^{-5}$ mol dm‚ĀĽ¬≥
5. $pH = -\log(1.05 \times 10^{-5}) = \mathbf{4.98}$

### Basic Salts (SB-WA)
Formed from weak acid + strong base. pH > 7.

- **Anion undergoes hydrolysis** (accepts proton from water)
- Examples: CH‚āÉCOONa, KNO‚āā

$$\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}_{(aq)} + \text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{COONa}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)}$$

Dissociation:
$$\text{CH}_3\text{COONa}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+_{(aq)} + \text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-_{(aq)}$$

Hydrolysis:
$$\text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-_{(aq)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons \text{CH}_3\text{COOH}_{(aq)} + \text{OH}^-_{(aq)}$$

```smiles
CC(=O)[O-].[Na+]
```
```smiles
[K+].[O-]N=O
```
#### Worked Example: pH of Sodium Benzoate Solution
0.05 mol C‚āÜH‚āÖCOONa dissolved in 100 cm¬≥ volumetric flask. $K_a$ for C‚āÜH‚āÖCOOH = $6.5 \times 10^{-5}$.

1. $[\text{C}_6\text{H}_5\text{COONa}] = \frac{0.05 \times 1000}{100} = 0.5$ mol dm‚ĀĽ¬≥
2. $K_b = \frac{K_w}{K_a} = \frac{1.0 \times 10^{-14}}{6.5 \times 10^{-5}} = 1.54 \times 10^{-10}$
3. ICE table for C‚āÜH‚āÖCOO‚ĀĽ hydrolysis:
   - Initial: [C‚āÜH‚āÖCOO‚ĀĽ] = 0.5, [C‚āÜH‚āÖCOOH] = 0, [OH‚ĀĽ] = 0
   - Change: ‚Äďx, +x, +x
   - Equilibrium: (0.5 ‚Äď x), x, x
4. $K_b = \frac{x^2}{0.5-x} \approx \frac{x^2}{0.5}$
5. $x = [\text{OH}^-] = 8.77 \times 10^{-6}$ mol dm‚ĀĽ¬≥
6. $pOH = -\log(8.77 \times 10^{-6}) = 5.06$
7. $pH = 14 - 5.06 = \mathbf{8.94}$ (> 7, basic salt)

### Salts of Weak Acid & Weak Base (WA-WB)
Both cation and anion hydrolyse in water. The salt may be neutral, acidic, or basic depending on relative $K_a$ and $K_b$:

| Condition | Salt Type | Dominant Mechanism |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| $K_a \approx K_b$ | Neutral | Cation and anion hydrolysis equally extensive |
| $K_a > K_b$ | Acidic | Cation hydrolysis more extensive |
| $K_a < K_b$ | Basic | Anion hydrolysis more extensive |

**Example:** C‚āÜH‚āÖCOONH‚āĄ (ammonium benzoate) is an **acidic salt** because $K_a$ for C‚āÜH‚āÖCOOH ($6.5 \times 10^{-5}$) > $K_b$ for NH‚āÉ ($1.8 \times 10^{-5}$).

```smiles
O=C([O-])c1ccccc1.[NH4+]
```
**Example:** CH‚āÉCOONH‚āĄ (ammonium ethanoate) is a **neutral salt** because $K_a$ for CH‚āÉCOOH ($1.8 \times 10^{-5}$) = $K_b$ for NH‚āÉ ($1.8 \times 10^{-5}$).

```smiles
CC(=O)[O-].[NH4+]
```
